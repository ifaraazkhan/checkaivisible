# Marketing & Product Strategy — CheckAIVisible

> Founder + marketing synthesis. The strategic case for whether this is a real
> product, who it's actually for, how the funnel works, how to rank credibly,
> and how to validate cheaply before burning cash.

---

## Bottom line up front

The pain is real and the category is hot, but **the consumer leaderboard is not
the product — it's the acquisition layer.** The actual product is "help a
business get recommended by AI," and the leaderboard is the Trojan horse to reach
those businesses cheaply.

- Stay clear-eyed about that → it's worth a **small, time-boxed bet**.
- Think the public ranking itself is the business → you'll burn cash on traffic
  that doesn't convert.

**One-liner verdict:** This is a real product in a hot category, but it's a
*distribution and trust* business wearing a *data* costume. The public
leaderboard is brilliant **as a customer-acquisition + SEO engine for a B2B
"get-recommended-by-AI" tool** — and weak as a standalone consumer destination.

---

## 1. Is the idea real / visible?

Yes — but know which wave you're riding.

There's a genuine gold rush around **AI visibility / GEO / answer-engine
optimization**. Profound, Ahrefs Brand Radar, Peec, Otterly, Scrunch — real money
is flowing in, which means **the underlying pain is already validated for you.**
Businesses are watching AI eat their search traffic and they're scared they're
invisible in the answer that replaces the ten blue links. You don't have to
educate the market that this matters — most startups die doing exactly that.

**The catch:** it's crowded and incumbents have distribution (Ahrefs could flip
this on for millions of existing users tomorrow). So the edge can't be "we check
AI visibility too." The edge is **format + distribution**:

> Public, free, consumer-framed, SEO-compounding, shareable — the
> **G2 / SimilarWeb of AI recommendations**, not another B2B dashboard behind a
> login.

That's a wedge incumbents structurally won't copy fast, because their model is
paywalled dashboards.

---

## 2. Do users actually care about the ranking?

Split this into two people — they are **not** the same user.

### The consumer / buyer (someone searching "best CRM") — WEAK
They will not bookmark your leaderboard. Why read your list of what AI recommends
when they can just ask the AI directly? **Don't build for this person as a
retention play.** They're SEO traffic passing through, nothing more.

### The business owner / founder / marketer — STRONG, and emotional
This is the real user, and the emotion is **ego + fear**:

- "Am I on the list? Where? Below my competitor?!"
- "I'm not on the list at all — why? How do I fix it?"

That second reaction **is the entire business.** Founders are vain about rankings
and terrified of being invisible. Show someone:

> "Your competitor appears in 19 of 25 AI answers for *project management*. You
> appear in 2."

…and you've created a wound they'll pay to close. **The leaderboard's job is to
manufacture that wound at scale, for free, via SEO.**

**Verdict:** consumers don't care, businesses *desperately* do. Build the whole
funnel around the business; dress it up as a consumer trust ledger for
distribution.

---

## 3. The funnel

The instinct (check domain → rank → list them → they share) is the right
skeleton. Tightened so each step actually converts:

1. **Top of funnel = programmatic SEO.** Pages like "Best [category], according
   to AI (2026)" that rank for "best X" queries. Free, compounding traffic — the
   one durable moat. *This is where cash should go, not into more engines/prompts.*

2. **The hook on the page = the ego trigger.** Every leaderboard page has a
   "Check your business" box: *"Don't see your business? Check where AI ranks you →"*

3. **Free check = email capture + a real report.** Show which engines mention
   them, appearance rate, who's beating them, **and one concrete reason**
   ("you're absent from comparison-style prompts"). The report must sting a
   little and be genuinely useful — that's what earns the email and the share.

4. **Listing them publicly = the viral act.** Give them an **embeddable badge** —
   "Recommended by AI · #3 in [category]." This is the G2 badge playbook and the
   best growth loop in B2B: the badge links **back to your site** = free
   backlinks = SEO compounds = you rank higher = more founders find you.
   Self-reinforcing.

5. **Retention = category subscription + change alerts.** "Your rank dropped this
   week" / "A new competitor entered your category." The only thing that brings
   the business back weekly. Also the natural paywall.

6. **Monetization = the fix, not the data.** Free tells them they're invisible.
   Paid tells them **how to fix it** + monitoring + competitor tracking + alerts.
   People pay to solve the anxiety you created in step 3.

**Key reframe:** the leaderboard is marketing; the alerts + "how to fix it" is
the product you charge for.

## 4. How to rank without torching credibility

A ranking system lives or dies on trust, and false precision is how trust dies
(the #4 who got beaten by #3 on noise will scream, publicly). **Tier, don't
over-number.**

- **Top of list: show ordinal rank (#1–#10)** where appearance rate is high and
  confident. These are your shareable ego hits — badges, "we're #2" tweets.
  Precision is *earned* and *valuable* here.

- **Middle / long tail: show tiers / bands, not numbers** — "Frequently
  recommended / Sometimes / Rarely / Not recommended." Kills the "why am I #47
  not #44" complaints while staying useful and honest.

- **Always expose the underlying number** — "appears in 18 of 25 AI answers."
  That's the methodology anchor and the defense against "this is made up." Raw
  appearance rate is more defensible than any computed rank.

- **Sparse / low-confidence categories: refuse to rank.** Show "Emerging — not
  enough data yet." Inventing a rank you can't defend earns a
  random-number-generator reputation. Restraint *is* the trust signal.

**Meta-principle:** rank only as precisely as the data honestly supports, and
make the methodology visible. That's the line between "credible public index"
and "SEO spam list nobody trusts."

## 5. Brutal-truth risks (say these out loud)

- **Distribution is the whole game, and it's the hard part.** The tech is trivial
  — call three APIs, aggregate, render. That means it's **not a tech moat**. You
  win or lose on SEO + virality + brand. If the programmatic pages don't rank and
  the badge loop doesn't spin, no engineering saves it.

- **Convert-to-paid is the real risk, not cost.** Everyone looks for free; far
  fewer pay. Validate that businesses will pay for "how to fix it" *before*
  scaling.

- **Methodology attacks.** The moment you have traffic, someone games it or
  accuses you of bias. Defense = transparent, conservative, appearance-rate-based
  scoring. Bake it in from day one.

- **Platform / ToS fragility.** Non-deterministic outputs, API changes, scraping
  risk on ChatGPT / AI Overviews. Build for it; don't be surprised.

- **One-and-done traffic.** Without alerts/subscription, this is a novelty people
  check once. The retention loop isn't optional — it's the difference between a
  viral toy and a business.

---

## 6. "Should I burn cash?" — reframe what the cash is

The API bill is **not** the real spend or the real risk — it's controllable and
small (cap it, sample fewer categories, cache). The real spend is **time +
distribution effort.** The right move is a **time-boxed validation bet, not a
full build:**

1. Pick **5–10 categories** you can speak to.
2. Ship the **programmatic SEO pages** + "check your domain" capture + the **badge**.
3. For ~6–8 weeks watch **two numbers only**:
   - Do the pages rank / get organic clicks?
   - Does the free check convert to emails + shares?
4. If both work, the loop is real → *then* scale categories and spend. If not,
   you've spent almost nothing and learned the distribution bet doesn't land.

**Don't** pour cash into more engines or more prompts (3 engines × 5 runs × ~20
prompts is already enough — see methodology notes). Pour validation into
**distribution + the conversion loop**, because that's the part that's actually
uncertain.

## Appendix — methodology stance (carried from prior decisions)

- **3 engines (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity) × 5 runs** is the right launch
  config. "n LLMs" on competitor sites is mostly a vanity metric — track the
  engines people actually ask. Design the schema for n engines from day one; add
  on demand signal, not competitor envy.
- **~20 prompts per category is enough** *if* they span intent archetypes
  (direct ranking, use-case qualified, comparison, constraint-driven,
  conversational). Competitors' "thousands of prompts" is a platform-wide library
  across all categories, not per-category — you'll have thousands too once you
  cover 60+ categories. Curated-and-mapped is a stronger trust position than raw
  volume.
- Audit the 20 for **archetype coverage**, not raw count. Tier later: head
  categories get more prompts/runs, long-tail get fewer.
